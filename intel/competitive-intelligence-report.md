## 1. RIDE Statewide Student Transportation Background

### Program Overview
RIDE's Statewide Transportation System (STS) provides home-to-school transportation for **3,400 students** across approximately **40 Local Education Agencies (LEAs)**. The system is presently divided into **7 zones** (A1, A2, A3, A4, B, C, D) and serves students attending public, private, parochial, and charter schools, plus career and technical education (CTE) centers and special education programs. The system debuted in 2009.

Rhode Island has the **largest out-of-district radius of any state, spanning more than 18 miles**. The zone map dates to 1977 and was originally designed around fewer CTE facilities. Rhode Island now has 14 districts offering CTE programs.

### 2024 Student Transportation Workgroup / Legislative Commission

In June 2024, the RI General Assembly created a **Special Joint Legislative Commission to Study Student Transportation Needs and System Costs**. The 13-member commission included six General Assembly members plus representatives from unions, school districts, state agencies, and a student representative. Led by Sen. Linda Ujifusa (D-Portsmouth), the commission began meeting in August 2024 and convened for the last time on April 7, 2025.

**Key findings:**
- Current five-region structure is based on an **outdated 1977 map of vocational high schools**
- **550 "ghost riders"** -- students who sign up for statewide bus service but never take the bus -- exert phantom demand on the system
- Routes cross multiple zones inefficiently, resulting in half-empty buses and long deadhead times

**Key recommendations:**
- Restructure from 5 regions to **9 smaller transportation zones** -- this could eliminate ~30 buses from the fleet and save **$3.5 million annually**
- Expand passenger van usage (cheaper than minibuses by ~$26,000 per vehicle; potential savings of **$750,000/year**)
- Remove ghost riders from transportation rolls
- Improve utilization of RIPTA (Rhode Island Public Transit Authority) routes
- Create subsidies for homeless and foster care student transportation
- Allow temporary driver licensing for properly licensed MA and CT drivers

**Legislative outcome (2025 session):** The General Assembly **declined to take up the zone restructuring bill** in 2025. The State Senate pulled the legislation from the Education Committee agenda on May 7, 2025. However, three other bills from the commission's recommendations **were signed into law** (see Section 4).

## 2. Incumbent Research

### 2A. First Student (Zones A1, A3)

**Company profile:**
- **Largest student transportation provider in North America**: 43,000 school buses, ~5 million students daily
- Operates in 38 states and 7 Canadian provinces
- **Headquarters:** Cincinnati, Ohio
- **Owner:** EQT Infrastructure (acquired in July 2021 for **$4.6 billion** from FirstGroup)
- Ranked #4,584 on 2025 Inc. 5000 list
- Received **$200M in EPA grant funding** (May 2024) for electric school bus transition
- Drove over **4 million electric miles** as of 2024-25; targeting 6 million by end of school year
- **Goal:** Transition 30,000 diesel buses to electric by 2035

**Rhode Island operations:**
- First Student is the **largest yellow bus company in Rhode Island**, serving most of the state's school districts for municipal/district-level contracts
- Currently holds RIDE statewide **Zones A1 and A3** (1,538 students, 151 routes combined)
- **Took over 31+ additional routes from Dattco** in September 2024 when Dattco failed to cover its assigned routes in the first two weeks of school -- First Student will keep those routes for the remainder of the 3-year contract
- In March 2023, First Student Providence drivers reached a new union contract making them among the **highest-paid school bus drivers** in the region

**Assessment:** First Student is deeply entrenched in Rhode Island. They were essentially the backup when Dattco failed, which strengthens their negotiating position. Their EV leadership (HALO platform, electric miles) could be a differentiator for the 2027 RFP given the new video monitoring mandate and state environmental goals. Union relationships through Teamsters Local 251 are strong.

### 2B. Dattco (Zones A2, A4, B, C)

**Company profile:**
- **Headquarters:** New Britain, Connecticut
- **Founded:** 1924 (originally as MASTCO by Ruth Mutter); now a **third-generation DeVivo family enterprise**
- **Revenue:** ~$653 million (per Dun & Bradstreet)
- **Employees:** 1,200+
- **Fleet:** 1,200+ buses across New England
- Transports **145,000+ students daily**
- **19 terminal/office/service facilities** throughout New England
- Three divisions: School Bus, Motor Coach Group (Megabus, charters), and Sales & Service
- **Non-union** for most school bus operations -- a key political liability in RI

**Rhode Island operations:**
- Awarded a **3-year, $20 million statewide contract** in May 2024, taking over Zones A2, A4, B, and C
- **Disastrous start to 2024-25 school year:**
  - Many Dattco drivers were **CT-based and lacked proper RI school bus credentials**
  - Over 30 routes had planned "double runs" (one driver, two routes), causing students to arrive **1 hour 41 minutes late on average**
  - **Children with disabilities were stranded** -- left at schools without pickup or not picked up at all
  - RIDE issued a **corrective action plan** to Dattco in September 2024
  - **41 total routes were transferred back to First Student** for the remainder of the contract
  - ACLU and RI Center for Justice demanded emergency action
  - Governor's office considered (but did not issue) an executive order to allow emergency out-of-state driver deployment

**Labor concerns:**
- Dattco offered wages and benefits **significantly below existing union contracts**
- Dattco allegedly sent text messages to unionized First Student workers offering **$3,000 bonuses to switch**
- RI AFL-CIO (representing Teamsters Local 251, 1199 SEIU, LiUNA Locals, UFCW Local 328, AFSCME Council 94, ATU Local 618) publicly opposed the contract award
- These unions represent **70% of the 2,500 school bus workers** in Rhode Island

**Assessment:** Dattco is politically damaged in Rhode Island. The September 2024 crisis generated significant media coverage, ACLU involvement, and legislative scrutiny. Their non-union model is a liability in a strong-union state. However, they remain a large New England operator with substantial infrastructure. They will almost certainly bid again, but they enter the 2027 procurement cycle weakened.

## 4. RI School Bus Legislation (2025)

Three school bus bills were passed into law in 2025, all stemming from the Student Transportation Workgroup/Commission recommendations:

### 4A. Increased Van Size (Pupil Transportation Vehicle)
- **What it does:** Amends the definition of "pupil transportation vehicle" from a maximum of **8 passengers to 10 passengers** plus the operator
- **RIDE's intent:** Maximize usage of these larger vans for statewide transportation whenever possible and practical
- **Cost impact:** Vans are ~$26,000 cheaper than minibuses. Replacing a few dozen minibuses with vans could save ~$750,000/year
- **Current fleet:** RI's school transit fleet now uses 150 vans
- **Implication for bidders:** Proposals should emphasize van-based routing strategies and demonstrate ability to deploy 10-passenger vans efficiently

### 4B. Temporary School Bus Driver Licenses
- **What it does:** Abbreviates the 10-hour training requirement for applicants who already hold a valid CDL; authorizes RI DMV to issue **temporary school bus driver licenses** to persons residing in **states bordering Rhode Island** (MA, CT) who meet other requirements
- **Why it matters:** Directly addresses the driver shortage that caused the Dattco crisis. Enables companies with cross-border operations (CT-based Dattco, MA-based operators) to legally deploy drivers in RI more quickly
- **Implication for bidders:** Companies with multi-state driver pools can now leverage that as a strength rather than a liability

### 4C. Live Digital Video Violation Detection Systems
- **Bill numbers:** H5421A / S0568A (signed by Governor McKee)
- **Sponsors:** Senate Majority Whip David P. Tikoian and Rep. Thomas E. Noret
- **Requirements:**
  - All **new school buses** must be equipped with live digital video school bus violation detection monitoring systems by **July 1, 2027** (coincides with new contract start!)
  - **All buses (new and used)** must have systems installed by **July 1, 2032**
  - Outward-facing video systems to monitor traffic violations (e.g., passing a stopped bus with flashing red lights)
- **Fines:** $250 first offense, up to $500 subsequent offenses (no license points)
- **Revenue distribution:** 30% to municipality, 30% to technology vendor, 40% to state general fund
  - Exception: Providence and Cumberland pre-existing contracts: 75% vendor, 12.5% city, 12.5% state
- **Enforcement results (2024-25 pilot):** Over **7,000 violations** issued between Oct 1, 2024 and June 12, 2025. Providence estimates ~$300,000 in revenue for FY2025.
- **Jurisdiction:** Shared between municipal courts and traffic tribunal
- **Implication for bidders:** All new buses in the 2027 fleet MUST have these systems. This is a significant capital/technology requirement. Companies should have partnerships with video monitoring vendors (BusPatrol, Verra Mobility, etc.) or in-house capability.

### Zone Restructuring (NOT passed)
- The proposal to restructure from 5 to 9 regions was **pulled from the Senate Education Committee** on May 7, 2025 and not heard during the 2025 session
- However, the **RFI itself asks about zone restructuring**, suggesting RIDE may pursue this administratively through the RFP process rather than legislatively
- Private school transportation was a politically sensitive issue -- Bishop Hendricken and other private schools lobbied against changes

## 6. Political Context

### The Dattco Crisis as Political Catalyst

The September 2024 Dattco service failure was the single biggest political event in RI school transportation in recent years. Key political impacts:

1. **ACLU involvement** -- The ACLU of Rhode Island and the RI Center for Justice sent formal letters to RIDE Commissioner Angelica Infante-Green demanding immediate corrective action, framing the bus failures as a civil rights issue (particularly for students with disabilities)

2. **Executive order debate** -- The Governor's office considered issuing an emergency executive order to allow out-of-state drivers. RIDE ultimately said it was not necessary after transferring 41 routes to First Student.

3. **Legislative commission creation** -- The crisis accelerated the already-planned legislative study commission, giving it urgency and public attention

4. **Union mobilization** -- The RI AFL-CIO coalition (representing 70% of the state's 2,500 school bus workers) used the Dattco failure to argue that non-union operators endanger students and undercut worker standards

### Union / Labor Dynamics

This is a **critical political factor** for any bidder:

- **Teamsters Local 251** is the dominant school bus union in RI and has secured landmark legislation:
  - **Prevailing wage standards** for school bus drivers
  - **Guaranteed 180 paid school days** annually
  - Protection from arbitrary dismissal
  - **Video monitoring systems** requirement (championed by Teamsters)
- In December 2025, Teamsters won a new contract with an **average 23% wage increase over three years**, plus Teamsters pension/healthcare, paid holidays, and attendance/safety bonuses
- The broader RI AFL-CIO coalition includes: Teamsters 251, 1199 SEIU, LiUNA Locals 808/1033/1322, UFCW 328, AFSCME Council 94, ATU Local 618
- Matt Taibi, Secretary-Treasurer of Teamsters Local 251, is the key labor figure

**Implication:** Any bidder that is perceived as anti-union or that proposes wages below prevailing standards will face significant political opposition. Dattco's experience is the cautionary tale.

### Private School Transportation Politics

- The commission's proposal to restructure zones also threatened to reduce/change transportation for private school students
- **Bishop Hendricken** and other private schools publicly opposed changes
- The zone restructuring bill was ultimately pulled -- private school lobby was reportedly a factor
- **52 students** from Bristol-Warren area alone are transported to private schools across Region 3
- A **grandfather clause** was proposed to ease transition (current private school students protected until July 1, 2029)

## 7. Strategic Implications for Bid

### What RIDE Is Signaling

Based on the RFI questions and political context, RIDE is signaling several priorities:

1. **Zone restructuring is coming** -- The RFI explicitly asks about zone structures, sub-zones, and multi-vendor awards. Even though legislation failed, RIDE can restructure zones administratively through the RFP.

2. **Barriers to entry matter** -- RIDE is asking what prevents companies from bidding, what support the state could provide (yard space, fuel, maintenance). They want more competition and are willing to provide infrastructure.

3. **Partnership model** -- "Develop...Partnership between state and vendors" is listed as a goal. The centralized routing authority / Statewide System Manager concept is already in place and will likely be strengthened.

4. **Cost transparency** -- The RFI asks detailed methodology questions about cost per bus per day, cost per mile, spare ratios, and driver hours per route. RIDE wants to understand cost structures, not just bottom-line pricing.

5. **Van maximization** -- The new van size law (10 passengers) plus RIDE's stated intent to "maximize the usage of these vehicles" means the RFP will likely reward van-heavy fleet proposals.

6. **Video monitoring readiness** -- All new buses must have live digital video systems by July 1, 2027 (day one of the new contract). This is a hard requirement.

7. **Driver pipeline is priority #1** -- The Dattco crisis made driver availability the top concern. The RFI timeline section specifically acknowledges the "national driver shortage" and asks about recruitment pipelines.

### Key Risks for Any Bidder

- **Union compliance:** Rhode Island's prevailing wage law and Teamsters strength mean non-union or below-market bids will face political headwinds
- **Driver licensing:** Even with the new temporary license law, RI still has complex licensing requirements. Any bidder must demonstrate RI-credentialed driver capacity from day one.
